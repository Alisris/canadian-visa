Canadian Public Holidays: What Do They Mean?

by 
Shireen Fisher
×

Author:

Shireen Fisher

BTech Degree in Journalism, Diploma in Journalism

Shireen Fisher is a widely-published writer with nine years of experience. She started her media career in radio before heading back to university to pursue her National Diploma and BTech Degree in Journalism.

. Updated: November 1st, 2023
6 min

The land of maple leaves is a country renowned for its rich cultural heritage and diverse traditions. One aspect that truly reflects this diversity is its public holidays. Also known as statutory holidays, these days are great for getting some much-needed rest and celebrating the holiday's significance. They are significant to Canadians, serving as a time for reflection and unity. As a future newcomer to Canada, you should learn about the calendar of events and dates to be aware of how you can celebrate them. Learn more about Canadian public holidays and what they mean before immigrating to Canada.

Exploring the Meaning Behind Canada's Major Public Holidays

Canada Day: Celebrating the Nation's Birthday

Canada Day is one of the biggest public holidays in Canada. It is celebrated on July 1st and is a day where Canadians come together to honor the country's birthday. It marks the anniversary of the Confederation of Canada, which occurred in 1867. Canadians proudly display their patriotism through various festivities, including concerts, fireworks, and flag-raising ceremonies.

Learn more about Canada Day.

Thanksgiving: A Time for Gratitude and Harvest

Thanksgiving in Canada is a time to express gratitude for the bountiful harvest and blessings received throughout the year. Celebrated on the second Monday in October, this holiday brings families and friends together for a traditional meal featuring roasted turkey, cranberry sauce, and pumpkin pie. It is also a time to reflect on the importance of giving thanks and helping those less fortunate.

Learn more about Thanksgiving in Canada.

Christmas and New Year: Embracing the Holiday Spirit

Christmas and New Year are celebrated enthusiastically and joyfully across Canada. These holidays allow families to come together, exchange gifts, and create lasting memories. The festive spirit is evident in the beautifully decorated homes, streets adorned with lights, and the cheerful carols filling the air. Canadians embrace the warmth and joy of the holiday season, spreading love and goodwill to all.

Easter: Celebrating Renewal and Rebirth

Easter is a Christian holiday celebrated in Canada as a period of renewal and rebirth. It symbolizes the resurrection of Jesus Christ and the triumph of life over death. Canadians partake in various traditions, such as attending church services, organizing Easter egg hunts, and sharing meals with loved ones. It is a time of reflection and spiritual rejuvenation.

Victoria Day: Honoring Queen Victoria's Legacy

Victoria Day, observed on the Monday preceding May 25th, is a day to honor Queen Victoria's birthday and her contributions to Canada. This holiday marks the unofficial start of summer. Canadian traditions for Victoria Day include enjoying outdoor activities, fireworks displays, and parades. It is a time to celebrate the monarchy and reflect on the country's historical ties to the British Commonwealth.

National Day for Truth and Reconciliation

September 30 marks the National Day for Truth and Reconciliation. It is a day that honors the children who never came back from and the survivors of Canada's residential schools, along with their families and communities. This public acknowledgment of the tragic and painful history and ongoing impacts of residential schools is an important part of the country's reconciliation efforts. Canadians are encouraged to wear orange in honor of Survivors of residential schools.

Remembrance Day

On November 11th, Canadians take a moment to remember the men and women who have served and continue to serve the country in periods of war and peace.

After World War 1, a French woman, Madame E Guérin suggested that women and children in war-torn areas of France produce poppies to sell to support veterans of the armed forces. Distribution of the first batches happened in the Great White North in November of 1921, and the tradition stood the test of time. Wearing a poppy is a symbol of remembrance. It serves as a reminder of the blood-red flower which today continues to bloom in the former battlefields of France and Belgium.

How Public Holidays Impact Canadian Society and Culture

Public holidays play a significant role in shaping Canadian society and culture. They provide a sense of identity, unity, and shared values among Canadians. These holidays unite people, fostering a sense of community and strengthening social bonds. They also contribute to the country's economy through increased tourism, as visitors flock to Canada to experience its unique holiday traditions and customs.

Celebrating Canadian Public Holidays as a Newcomer to Canada

For newcomers to Canada, public holidays provide an opportunity to learn about Canadian traditions and integrate into the cultural fabric of the country. As an immigrant, you'll bring your holiday traditions and customs to the Great White North, adding your thread to enrich the cultural tapestry of this country. When you move to Canada, you’ll learn that Canadian public holidays offer a chance to connect with your new community, share your cultural heritage, and build bridges of understanding and appreciation.

Canadian public holidays reflect the nation's commitment to multiculturalism. As a country known for its diverse population, it celebrates holidays meaningful to various cultural and religious communities. Public holidays such as Diwali, Hanukkah, and Eid al-Fitr are recognized alongside traditional Canadian holidays, fostering a sense of inclusivity and respect for all citizens who move to Canada.

FAQs

I'd like to Immigrate to Canada. What is the Quickest Way to Do This?

If you are a skilled worker, you can immigrate to Canada via the Express Entry Program. Processing for this option takes approximately six months. Your Express Entry options are as follows:

  • The Federal Skilled Worker Program, which is for skilled workers with a degree from a recognized tertiary educational institution
  • The Canadian Experience Class, which is for skilled tradespeople with a recognized diploma or certified job-specific training
  • The Federal Skilled Trades Program, which is for skilled workers who've been living and working in the Great White North for a minimum of one year straight via a study permit, work permit, or Working Holiday Visa

Does Canada Celebrate England's King Charles's Birthday?

According to the Immigration Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC) website, a proclamation fixed Victoria Day as the date on which the Sovereign's birthday would be celebrated here. Although it wasn't explicitly assigned King Charles III's birthday, the proclamation deliberately stated the Sovereign's birthday.
