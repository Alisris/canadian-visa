Canadian Immigration: The Easy Way With RCIC Jimmy Park

by Nicky VZ. Updated: May 15th, 2020

Do you want to immigrate to Canada? Choosing to relocate to a different country with or without family is a big life decision and one that can come with a few bumps in the road if you’re not well versed in Canadian immigration policies and procedures. That is why it's safer and easier to work with a Regulated Canadian Immigration Consultant (RCIC) right from the start to have a stress-free and successful journey to Canada.

But how do you choose the right professional to guide you through the complicated immigration process? Unfortunately, there are a variety of Canadian visa scams and fraudulent agencies that lie, cheat, and steal from hopefuls every year. For this reason, it’s important to educate yourself on how to spot if you are dealing with a legitimate RCIC or not.

In this blog, we look at 5 important things you can ask your immigration consultant to determine if they are fake or real. It’s also our pleasure to introduce RCIC Jaehyun (Jimmy) Park, who works for three highly esteemed and recommended agencies namely; Multi-Dimensional Consulting (MDC) and its sister company, CanadianVisa.Org as well as JP Immigration Consulting.

RCIC Jaehyun (Jimmy) Park

First Things First - What Is an RCIC?

Would you trust an unlicensed pilot to get you to your destination safely? We didn't think so. So, why trust an unlicensed immigration representative with something as important as your future? Regulated Canadian Immigration Consultants (RCICs) are fully accredited and authorized by the Immigration Consultancy of Canada Regulatory Council (ICCRC). That means they can legally charge a fee to represent you and take care of the entire visa and immigration process on your behalf. That’s right, you can take it easy while they strategize and take care of the mountain of paperwork.

5 Things to Ask Your Canadian Immigration Consultants




1. At Which Canadian Institution Did You Study?

RCICs are required to complete an accredited program such as an Immigration Diploma at one of the nine ICCRC approved post-secondary institutions in Canada and nowhere else. That means if the RCIC does not mention any of these eligible institutions you should think twice whether you’re dealing with a real or fake immigration consultant. The eligible institutions include:

  • Ashton College;
  • Academy of Learning Career College;
  • Anderson College of Health, Business, and Technology;
  • Bow Valley College;
  • CDI College;
  • Herzing College;
  • LaSalle College;
  • The University of British Columbia.

RCIC Jimmy Park enrolled at Ashton College in Vancouver after immigrating to Canada from South Korea and graduated with an Accredited Immigration Practitioner Program (IPP).

After successfully completing an IPP the RCIC applicant must write and pass an RCIC EPE examination. Then only are they qualified and licensed by the ICCRC and given RCIC registration numbers to practice. To keep them sharp and masters of their craft, RCICs are required to complete 16 hours of Continuing Professional Development (CPD) annually by participating in a number of ICCRC approved activities about Canadian immigration law. This is important seeing as immigration policies change regularly.

2. What is Your Registration Number?

Avoid immigration scams by asking for your immigration consultants' registration number. When you have his or her registration number you can easily verify their credentials online. It will tell you if your immigration consultant is licensed, actively practicing, and which agencies they are associated with. It’s important to know which agencies your consultant works with because there are con artists who steal real RCICs identifications.

3. Which Areas Do You Specialize In?


There are various ways to live, study, and work in Canada. In fact, there are currently 80+ immigration and visa categories that you probably never knew existed. It is the job of an immigration consultant to strategize a qualifying program for you and your family to come to Canada. However, an immigration agency or consultant can never guarantee your visa or application success; so if an agency advertises that you can “win” or “buy” a visa, steer clear of these Canadian immigration scams.

In addition, it can be that your immigration consultant chooses to specialize in specific immigration categories. For this reason, it’s good to tell them about your goals and learn if they can help you achieve them. For example, if you want to open a business in Canada then you need the professional help of a consultant who specializes in the Start-Up Visa Program and other business immigration programs.

RCIC Jaehyun (Jimmy) Park has more than 7 years of work experience in Canadian immigration and is an all-rounder when it comes to specialization. Not only does he cover the most popular immigration categories such as Express Entry, student, and work permits but he also assists Canadian businesses in obtaining their LMIA documents in order to hire foreign nationals. Furthermore, he is skilled in rehabilitation. That means if you had past refusals, he can develop a new strategy on how to submit your application and explain how your history of refusals may affect the chances of success.

Here is a list of RCIC Jaehyun (Jimmy) Park Canadian immigration specialization categories:

  • Express Entry Immigration;
  • Provincial Nomination Programs (PNPs);
  • Family Sponsorship;
  • Study Permits;
  • Work Permits;
  • Temporary Visa (Tourist Visa);
  • Permanent Residency Visa;
  • Rehabilitation.

4. Is Your Agency Recommended by Canadian Visa Review?

Canadian Visa Review (CVR) is a reliable source of information if you want to know more about an immigration agency, whether they can be trusted and if they work with real immigration consultants. CVR has finely combed the websites of most Canadian visa agencies and rated their services to make it easier for you to find the right agency. Discover if your immigration consultant’s associated agency has a good track record. Check the top 10 highest rated agencies here.

5. Which Immigration Program is Right For Me?


An immigration consultant should be able to tell you which program is best for you and why. However, in order to do this, the consultant will need to know details about your work background, education, and experience as well as your country of origin and your goals. Whether you want to move to Canada with family or not or if you already have family in Canada, etc. This part is also known as the comprehensive eligibility evaluation.

After you have completed your evaluation it will be sent to the RCIC who will use this information to determine your total Comprehensive Ranking System (CRS) points (immigration points) and strategize an immigration program that you qualify for. This process can take approximately 2-weeks depending on your CRS score. However, if an immigration consultant tells you which program you qualify for immediately and without a thorough analysis first, it can mean that you’re facing immigration fraud.
