If you thought skilled trades only meant carpentry, welding, plumbing, and auto mechanics, think again. Trade is much more diverse than we give recognition to, and includes occupations such as network cabling technicians, special events coordinators, bakers, computer hardware technicians, educational assistants, and so many others. To pursue your trade you will need some form of qualification, and what better place to obtain one than in Canada? Here are some trade schools in Canada and how to get there.

Top Schools For Trade In Canada

According to Canada’s government, the top five in-demand trades in Canada are carpenters, cooks, hair stylists, auto service technicians, and welders. Here are some of the best colleges for each of these trades.

Carpenters

Carpenters have a very broad field that they work in. They can do anything from installing kitchen cabinets to building bridges and highways. Most carpentry courses will cover blueprint reading; machine woodworking; geometry; physics; hand tool selection, care and use; architectural drawing; identification and measurement of materials; project management, and estimating.

Carpenters in Canada can make up to 48,946 CAD annually or 25.10 CAD per hour on average. Beginners in the industry usually start with a salary of 39,139 CAD per year, while more experienced workers can make up to 60,450 CAD per year.

All our salary expectations are taken from Talent.com and are subject to change based on your experience and the specific job your acquire.

Finding a job in the food service industry in Canada is a recipe for success.

This is ideal work for a socialite as they will always meet and engage with new people daily.

One of the best things about studying to become a hairstylist is that you can do it full-time or part-time, which means you will have time to work while chasing your dreams. Requirements to Study a Trade in Canada

Studying a trade in Canada offers an excellent pathway to acquire practical skills and embark on a rewarding career in various industries. With a growing demand for skilled tradespeople nationwide, pursuing a trade education has become an attractive option for many individuals seeking hands-on training and direct entry into the workforce. However, like any educational pursuit, there are certain requirements aspiring trade students must meet to ensure a successful journey toward a fulfilling career.

Age and Education

To study a trade in Canada, students must typically be at least 18 years old and have completed their high school education or an equivalent diploma.

Language Proficiency

Proficiency in English or French is essential for trade programs in Canada. International students may need to demonstrate their language skills through standardized tests like IELTS, PTE or CELPIP.

Pre-Apprenticeship Programs

Many trade programs require students to complete pre-apprenticeship programs to gain foundational knowledge and skills before moving on to formal apprenticeships.

Physical Fitness

Certain trades, such as construction and welding, demand physical strength and stamina. Students may undergo a medical examination to ensure they can meet the physical demands of their chosen trade.

Criminal Background Check

Some trades, especially those involving public safety and security, may require students to pass a criminal background check.

Specific Program Requirements

Each trade program may have its own unique set of prerequisites, such as specific courses, work experience, or certifications.

Apprenticeship Agreements

For apprenticeship programs, students must secure an apprenticeship agreement with an employer to gain practical work experience alongside their classroom learning.

By meeting these requirements, aspiring trade students can set themselves on a rewarding career, contributing to the skilled workforce driving Canada's economy and infrastructure development.

Cost of Trade School in Canada

The cost of trade school in Canada is generally more affordable compared to traditional university education. Trade schools offer specialized training and practical skills development in a shorter duration, making them a cost-effective option for individuals seeking a quicker entry into the workforce. As seen above, each institution will have its own tuition range and focus.

Tuition fees for trade programs are often lower than university degrees, and students can save on additional expenses like room and board since many trade schools offer shorter, non-residential programs.

Furthermore, trade schools also provide opportunities for financial aid, scholarships, and bursaries to assist students in managing their education expenses. Various organizations, government agencies, and industry associations offer scholarships and bursaries to support aspiring trade students.

Additionally, students can work part-time or take up apprenticeships during their studies to earn income and gain hands-on experience simultaneously. This work-study approach helps offset the financial burden and offers practical learning opportunities that can further enhance their employability upon graduation.

Getting Licensed to Work Your Trade in Canada

In Canada, obtaining a license to work in your trade is a crucial step after completing your studies. The Red Seal Program plays a significant role in the skilled trades by providing a nationally recognized certification for qualified individuals.

The Red Seal endorsement signifies that a tradesperson has met the national standards of excellence in their trade. It allows skilled workers to practice their trade across different provinces and territories in Canada without needing to requalify or undergo additional assessments. This portability of credentials makes it easier for tradespeople to pursue employment opportunities in various regions, fostering mobility and career growth.

For certain trades like cooks and hairdressers, the requirements for obtaining a license may vary across provinces. Some provinces might have specific provincial certification exams or training requirements in addition to the Red Seal endorsement. For instance, cooks might need to pass a practical cooking exam or undergo a food safety certification. In contrast, hairdressers may need to complete a provincial licensing exam to demonstrate their skills and knowledge.

It's essential for individuals aspiring to work in these trades to research the specific licensing requirements in the province they wish to practice in.

Federal Skilled Trades Program (FSTP)

This program is one of many that is managed by the Express Entry system. It is designed to assist foreign workers with work experience in their desired fields. If you qualify as a skilled trade worker according to the federal government, you could apply to this program through Express Entry.

There requirements of the FSTP are:

  • A valid job offer from a Canadian employer (the offer has to be full time);
  • Language proficiency score in French or English determined by the Canadian Language Benchmark (CLB) or the Niveaux de compétence linguistique canadiens (NCLC);
  • More than 2 years worth of work experience;
  • Demonstrate that you can meet the job requirements and;
  • Proof of settlement funds for you and your family.

If you’re planning on immigrating to Canada as a skilled worker, then this would be the perfect option for you.

FAQS

What is the Main Difference Between Universities and Colleges in Canada?

Universities and Colleges in Canada are different. Universities in Canada grant degrees, often they do have courses that offer certificates or diplomas. They deal with academic and professional programs, while colleges usually focus on training and trades.

What are Federal Skilled Trades Program Processing Times?

Most applications for this program can be completed in up to six months.
